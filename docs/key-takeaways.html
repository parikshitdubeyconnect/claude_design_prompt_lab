01The six-part anatomy of a great prompt

A fantastic prompt covers all six. Use it as a checklist before you hit send.
Role
Who the AI should be — an analyst, an RM, an architect. Sets expertise and voice.
Context
The situation and audience — who's reading, what they already know.
Task
One clear, specific job to do.
Format
The exact structure you want back — sections, table, word limit.
Constraints / Tone
Length, register, and what to avoid. The dial that matters most for people-facing text.
Examples
A short sample of the standard you expect.

02What the exercises taught us

Rating the prompts

  1. Precision in, precision out. A vague prompt earns a vague answer — the model mirrors the quality of your ask.
  2. More isn't better. "Overcooked" prompts — piling on roles, contradictory limits ("20 tables in 150 words"), asking it to invent what's missing — backfire. Right-size the ask.
  3. Judge a prompt by its anatomy. The winner isn't the longest or fanciest; it's the one that's specific, structured, and honest about uncertainty.

Writing the prompt

  1. Anchor it to the actual task. Write the prompt in context to what's being asked or produced — the artefact, the reader, the decision it feeds.
  2. Specify the exact format — numbered sections, a table, a word limit; the shape you want back.
  3. Tell it what not to do. For BRDs, credit memos, legacy code: don't invent, don't resolve ambiguities, flag hidden rules with evidence, and say when data is stale rather than guess.
Tone is a business control, not a nicety
For customer- and front-facing communication the tonality you specify in the prompt drives how the output lands — warmth, formality, no jargon, no grovelling. Getting it wrong is a relationship risk, so state it explicitly. For internal, engineering or AI/developer-led prompts that same tonality matters far less — there, spend your words on precision, evidence and structure instead. In short: customer-facing → set the tone developer-led → optimise for precision

Overarching

  • Judgement stays with you. AI drafts, you decide. Keep a human in the loop for risk, compliance and client-facing work.
  • Governance first. Synthetic scenarios only — never enter client, personal or confidential data into consumer tools.

The reusable rule of thumb

  • Say who it is, who it's for, the one job, the shape of the answer, the limits, and show an example.
  • Then adjust one variable at a time — tone, or length, or format — rather than rewriting from scratch.

03What you can change day-to-day — copilots & LLMs

Small habits that lift quality immediately, whether you're in ChatGPT, Claude, Microsoft 365 Copilot or Salesforce Einstein.
  • Open with role + context in one line. "You are a credit analyst writing a 3-minute pre-read for the risk committee…"
  • State the format you want before you ask — bullets, a table, five numbered sections, under 180 words.
  • Add two constraints: one on tone (for people-facing text) and one on honesty — "neutral tone; where data is stale, say so — do not guess."
  • Give it the source and pin it there. Paste the memo/notes/code and say "use only the material provided; don't add anything."
  • Iterate by one variable. If the answer's off, change the format or the length or the tone — not everything at once.
  • Match effort to channel. Customer-facing → set tone explicitly and review before sending. Code/analysis → prioritise precision, evidence and citations over voice.
  • Ask for its assumptions and gaps. "List anything you were unsure about or had to assume" surfaces risk you'd otherwise miss.
  • Never paste client, personal or confidential data into a consumer tool — use the sanctioned enterprise copilot with the trust controls turned on.

04Guardrails as configuration — LLMs embedded in an OEM

When the LLM lives inside Salesforce (Einstein / Agentforce) or Microsoft Dynamics 365 & Copilot, most guardrails are platform configuration, not code. Set them centrally so end users inherit them and can't strip them.
Ground the model to sanctioned data Scope answers to approved objects/records only (Salesforce grounding & Data Cloud; Dynamics via Dataverse). Enforce row-/field-level security so the assistant inherits each user's permissions — least privilege, no elevated service accounts.
Own the instruction layer Bake role, tone and do/don't rules into the platform's prompt templates (Einstein Prompt Builder; Copilot Studio topics & instructions) so they're applied server-side and users can't override them.
Turn on the trust / DLP layer Salesforce Einstein Trust Layer: PII masking, toxicity detection, zero data-retention with the model vendor, full audit trail. Microsoft Purview DLP + sensitivity labels for Copilot; Azure OpenAI content filters set to the right severity.
Constrain the outputs Require grounding/citations, enable toxicity & grounding scoring, and configure the assistant to defer — "I don't have that information" — instead of hallucinating.
Human-in-the-loop for actions For anything customer-facing (case replies, emails) or that writes data, configure draft-only + approval workflow rather than auto-send. Scope connectors/agent actions to read-only where possible.
Tone & register by channel Maintain separate prompt templates per channel — a formal, regulated tone for customer comms; a terser, precision-first template for internal/developer assistance. (This is the tonality lesson, encoded as config.)
Audit & retention Enable prompt/response logging, set zero-retention with the LLM vendor, and apply your retention & monitoring policies. Review logs for drift and misuse.
Remember: the framework — Role · Context · Task · Format · Constraints/Tone · Examples — is the same whether you're typing into a chat box or configuring an enterprise copilot.
